The Business Problem
WHY THIS PROJECT EXISTS
Employee attrition costs 50–200% of annual salary per departure — in recruiting fees, onboarding, and lost productivity. Yet most HR teams only discover someone is leaving after they resign.

This platform analyses 19,158 data scientists who completed training programmes. Using 13 features — from city development index to years of experience — we train 5 ML models to predict which employees are actively seeking a new job right now, weeks before they apply elsewhere.

The output is a probability score per employee. HR can then prioritise high-risk individuals for targeted interventions — retention bonuses, promotions, or role redesign — before the resignation letter arrives.

Quick Insights
📌 Research-Backed Key Insights
Each finding is grounded in published research and validated by patterns in the dataset. These observations explain why the model works — connecting data science to HR psychology and organisational behaviour.
1
Experience vs Job Switching — The Honeymoon–Hangover Effect
Job satisfaction peaks right after a job change but declines over time, causing employees with <2 years of experience to show the highest switching rate (40–45%). In our data, "<1 year" candidates seek at 45.4% — nearly twice the dataset average.
↗ Honeymoon–Hangover Effect (Wikipedia)
2
Training & Attrition — Skill Development Increases Mobility
Training enhances employee skills and raises their external job market value, which can increase switching intentions — especially for those with heavy training investment. In the dataset, high-training-hour candidates (200+) show slightly lower seek rates (20.1%), suggesting that very invested employees may stay — but the relationship is nuanced.
↗ Employee Attrition: Causes & Consequences (ResearchGate)
3
Company Size Effect — Small Firms Lose More Talent
Smaller organisations experience higher employee turnover due to limited growth opportunities and job security concerns. The dataset shows employees in large firms (10,000+) seek at 19.1% while the pattern aligns with research showing stability at scale — though the relationship is non-linear.
↗ Employee Attrition Studies (ResearchGate)
4
Education Impact — Higher Qualifications, More Options
Employees with higher educational qualifications have more employment options and mobility. Graduates seek at 28.0% — the highest of any education level — while PhDs seek at only 14%, suggesting specialisation creates retention. This validates the "educated options" hypothesis.
↗ Employee Attrition Analysis (IJRPR)
5
Location & CDI — Workers Seek Better Economic Environments
Workers increasingly move toward regions offering better economic opportunities. The City Development Index is the #1 feature in this model. Employees in cities with CDI <0.65 seek at 58–59% — six times the rate of high-CDI cities (<11%). Economic environment is the strongest single predictor.
↗ McKinsey — The Great Attrition Report
6
Skill Development — Active Learners Seek Better Roles
Employees who actively invest in skill development are more likely to seek improved opportunities. University enrollment correlates with higher seek rates — full-time enrolled candidates (~32%) vs not enrolled (~22%). Self-investment signals external ambition.
↗ Attrition Behaviour Studies (ResearchGate)
7
Gender Impact — Weak Determinant of Job Switching
Job-switching behaviour is not strongly determined by gender, though outcomes differ slightly. Female candidates seek at 26.3%, male at 22.8%, and "Other" at 26.2% — differences exist but are modest. Gender ranks among the lowest feature importances in our models.
↗ Gender Pay & Mobility Study (arXiv)
8
Class Imbalance — The Core ML Challenge
Attrition datasets typically exhibit class imbalance — fewer employees leave than stay. At 24.9% positive rate, a naive model predicting "stay" always would achieve 75% accuracy but zero utility. We address this with class_weight='balanced' and threshold tuning, making F1-score and ROC-AUC the relevant metrics — not accuracy.
↗ ML Attrition Studies (arXiv)
🧠 Research Conclusion

These eight findings collectively validate that City Development Index, years of experience, company size and type, and active skill investment are the primary drivers of employee attrition — aligning both with the ML model's feature importances and published HR research. The dataset's patterns are not statistical noise — they reflect real organisational psychology documented across multiple academic and industry sources.
